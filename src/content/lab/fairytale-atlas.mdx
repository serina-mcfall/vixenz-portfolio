You fall through a rabbit hole into a misted watercolour map of the world. You roam it. When you wander close to a country, its tales come alive in that culture's own art tradition: a character turns, looks at you, and tells you their story. Underneath every tale, quietly, sits the real thing: where it comes from, what it means, and the other tales across the map that share its ache.

The dream sits on top. The encyclopedia sits underneath. You never feel the second one while you're falling.

Tales connect by meaning, not just geography. The forbidden-look thread crosses Japan and Greece. The oldest written Cinderella is Ye Xian, Tang dynasty, 850 CE, a thousand years before the European versions. When a wanderer discovers that on the map, it is the moment they feel the whole world has been telling each other the same handful of stories across every border and every century.

Ship one tradition deep and luminous. The rest of the map sits under mist that hasn't lifted yet: a magical in-world device and honest modular architecture at the same time.

First region: East Asia. First tale: the Cowherd and the Weaver Girl (牛郎织女), tied to Qīxī. The same lovers reappear as Tanabata in Japan and Chilseok in Korea, the same story wearing three countries' clothes.

Accessibility-first, built not patched. Dyslexia-friendly pacing, reduced-motion alternative for the falling transition.
